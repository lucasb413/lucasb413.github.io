Listening for new physics across the gravitational wave spectrum.

As an early-career researcher and science communicator, I aim to 'listen in' on the most energetic events in our universe using the newfound field of gravitational wave astronomy, translating these space-time-sound-waves into new insights about black holes, compact objects, dark matter, and cosmology.

Science Communication

Science can be complicated—but that shouldn't stop scientists from trying to make their work accessible to as many people as possible, both inside and outside of their field. The course of scientific progress directly impacts the lives of billions of people around the world, making access to scientific knowledge and trust between researchers and the general public an important societal goal. Plus, learning about science is fun! As an author at Astrobites, a graduate-student-run astronomy and astrophysics blog supported by the American Astronomical Society, I get to write about cutting-edge astrophysics research in a fun and casual way typically aimed at undergraduates but accessible to anyone with an interest in science.

My Recent Astrobites Articles:

A Light in the Dark: Looking for Bright Black Holes as Dark Matter

If extremely low-mass primordial black holes are lurking in our galaxy, they should glow white hot with Hawking radiation. Today’s authors explore whether we can use this fact to tease out their presence.

Rapid Microlensing Classification: A Lonely SOBH Story

Today’s paper explores how we can use simulations of stellar populations to rapidly estimate the identity of the lensing object in a microlensing event.

That’s No Moon! The Search for Black Hole Asteroids, Stars, and Planets

If primordial black holes exist, one place we might find them is in binary systems with other objects like planets or asteroids. Today’s paper explores how common these systems might be and the various ways we might go about finding them.

Black Hole, Why Don’t You Come Over? White Dwarf Supernova!

The origin of dim supernovae known as Ca-rich transients is still not well-understood. Could it be that these rare events are set off by an encounter between a white dwarf and a primordial black hole?

Black Holes All the Way Down: New Multimessenger Evidence for a Binary Black Hole Merger Within an Active Galactic Nucleus

In today's paper, a unique gravitational wave event is re-examined as the possible origin of an AGN flare. What are the odds?

Forget X-Ray Vision: Using Gravitational Waves to Peer Inside of the Sun

What if we could use continuous gravitational waves from far away pulsars to learn about the inside of our very own sun? The authors of today’s paper explore what it would take to make this far-out idea a reality.

Gardens of the Galaxy: Has JWST Confirmed the Existence of Heavy Supermassive Black Hole Seeds?

A recent observation of an extremely distant quasar sheds some (X-ray) light on the long-standing mystery of supermassive black hole formation.

Gravitational Lensing Unveils an Ultramassive Black Hole In the Heart of Abell 1201

Measuring the mass of black holes in the centers of far away galaxies can be tricky, but today’s paper demonstrates the viability of a new method in this field: gravitational lensing!

How Good are Humans at Visually Identifying Gravitational Lenses?

In an era of increasing reliance on computers to classify astrophysical imagery, what role do humans have to play? Today’s paper examines human biases in visual searches for gravitational lensing.

About Me

I was born and raised in Santa Cruz, CA; My proximity to UC Santa Cruz growing up likely helped instill in me a longstanding fascination with physics and astronomy. I moved a few hours up the road to UC Davis for my undergraduate education, ultimately graduating with a B.S. in physics with an astrophysics concentration and a minor in philosophy. Upon graduating I moved to Somerville, MA to pursue my master's in physics at Tufts University, which I obtained in May 2024. Starting in Fall 2024, I will be a PhD student at UC Santa Cruz, bringing my physics journey full circle! In my free time, I love to play piano, listen to music, go rock climbing, and hang out with my chihuahua Phoebe.
